# Empathy Map: Strategic Navigator (Nadia)

## Executive Summary

**Key Insights:**
1. **The legitimacy gap is deeper than the knowledge gap.** Nadia doesn't just lack strategy skills — she lacks the felt permission to do strategy. The impostor dimension is the strongest emotional force and the biggest adoption risk for the Coach agent.
2. **Curiosity flashes are the entry point.** When a framework suddenly makes something visible ("oh, THIS is what strategy feels like"), Nadia is briefly in the zone. The Coach's job is to trigger and sustain those moments — not teach strategy from a textbook.
3. **Strategy is endured, not owned.** The dominant post-strategy feeling is relief, not pride. The Coach must transform this from survival to ownership.
4. **Loneliness drives coping mechanisms.** The reverse-engineering trap, the gut-check call, the slide-deck-as-process — all are solo coping strategies for someone who has no strategic sparring partner.
5. **The say/think gap reveals defensiveness.** Nadia says "our product IS our strategy" (confident) but thinks "that should be enough" (defensive). She suspects it's not enough but can't safely explore what's missing.

**Primary Pain Points:**
1. Legitimacy void — not mandated for strategy daily, then ambushed by the demand
2. Reverse-engineering trap — strategy built backward from product, missing everything the product doesn't address
3. One strategy fits all — no diagnostic layer to match approach to situation

**Top Desired Gains:**
1. "I belong in this conversation" — ownership replaces survival mode
2. "I know what I don't know" — coverage awareness, no invisible blind spots
3. "This feels like thinking, not homework" — curiosity sustained, not template-filling

## Target User Profile

**Who:** Product lead at a Series A startup (15-30 people). Mid-30s. Technical background, promoted into product leadership. Smart, capable, but never formally trained in strategic analysis. Has fragments of strategy knowledge (business school module on Porter, blog post on Wardley, a SWOT attempt) but no integration framework and no confidence to act on it.

**Job-to-be-done:** When my product faces a critical decision — launch, pivot, competitive threat, or growth stall — I need to think through it strategically rather than reactively, so I make a decision I can defend to my team and stakeholders.

**Context:** Between sprint reviews and customer calls. Strategy gets 2 hours on a Thursday afternoon when she cancels a meeting. Her co-founder says "we need a strategy doc for the board" and she has 5 days. High cognitive load, high stress, low time, multiple stakeholders with opinions.

**Research Sources:**
- Expert observation: Amalik's direct consulting experience with product teams in this position
- NOT validated with direct novice research — this empathy map is a hypothesis requiring Isla-led validation
- Confidence calibration: the impostor dimension and curiosity flashes are high-confidence observations; the specific coping behaviors are medium-confidence inferences

## Says & Thinks

### What They SAY (Direct Quotes)

1. *"We need to be more strategic"* — says it in team meetings, means "we're reacting to everything." Uses the word without a clear picture of what it would look like.
2. *"Let me put together a deck for the board"* — defaults to the artifact (slides) rather than the thinking process. The deck IS the strategy.
3. *"Our product is our strategy — we're building something nobody else has"* — reverse-engineering. Genuine belief that product differentiation = strategic positioning.
4. *"I'm not sure this is really my area"* — said quietly to trusted colleague, never to the board. The legitimacy doubt surfacing.
5. *"Can you just tell me what framework to use?"* — to consultants. Wants the answer, not the process, because the process feels like someone else's job.

### What They THINK (Inferred Thoughts)

1. *"Everyone else in this room seems to know what 'strategy' means. I'm faking it."* — impostor syndrome specifically around strategic language and thinking.
2. *"If I ask too many questions about strategy, they'll realize I don't belong in this role."* — legitimacy fear prevents learning in public.
3. *"The board cares about growth numbers, not frameworks. Why do I need Porter?"* — genuine confusion about why strategic analysis matters when she has product metrics.
4. *"I built this product. I understand it better than anyone. That should be enough."* — reverse-engineering isn't just coping — it's a source of pride that feels threatened.
5. *"I'll figure it out when I really need to."* — deferral. Strategy feels like a problem for later, until the board meeting is Thursday.

**Say/Think Gap:** Statement #3 (says "product is strategy" — confident) contradicts Thought #4 (thinks "that should be enough" — defensive). She suspects it's not enough but doesn't have the vocabulary or permission to explore what's missing.

## Does & Feels

### What They DO (Observable Actions)

1. **Starts with the product, works backward** — opens roadmap or feature list, constructs a "strategy" narrative around what's already built or planned.
2. **Googles "strategy framework for startups"** — 45 minutes reading articles, opens 6 tabs, picks whichever framework the most credible blog recommends. Closes tabs, starts a deck.
3. **Builds slides, not thinking artifacts** — first action is creating a presentation, not structuring thought. Formatting substitutes for depth.
4. **Avoids strategy conversation until forced** — steers team meetings toward product updates, sprint velocity, customer feedback. Tactical territory where she's confident.
5. **Asks one trusted person for a gut check** — before presenting, calls a co-founder, advisor, or friend-who-did-an-MBA. "Does this make sense?" Reassurance, not structured feedback.

### What They FEEL (Emotional States)

1. **Impostor anxiety** (strongest, persistent) — low-grade hum whenever strategy comes up. Spikes sharply when board meetings are scheduled.
2. **Resentment** (surprising but real) — "Why is this my job? I was hired to build product." The mandate shift from builder to strategist wasn't chosen.
3. **Relief when it's over** — after presenting to board, dominant feeling isn't pride. It's "they didn't ask the hard question." Strategy is endured, not owned.
4. **Lonely** — nobody thinks alongside her. Team looks to her for direction. Board evaluates her. The gut-check call is the closest thing to strategic partnership.
5. **Flashes of genuine curiosity** — when a framework suddenly clicks, there's a moment of "oh, THIS is what strategy feels like." Brief. Unstructured. Not repeated because she doesn't know how to get back there.

**Does/Feels Connections:**
- DOES: builds slides first → FEELS: less anxious (artifact gives sense of progress)
- FEELS: lonely → DOES: gut-check call (seeking a moment of partnership)
- FEELS: flashes of curiosity → DOES: nothing (no process to capture or build on the moment)

## Pain Points

**Priority:** High = Critical | Medium = Important | Low = Nice-to-fix

**Pain #1: The Legitimacy Void** *(Emotional — HIGH)*
- **The pain:** Not mandated for strategy daily, then suddenly expected to deliver it. No gradual ramp-up, no permission structure.
- **Impact:** Approaches strategy from doubt rather than ownership. Everything produced carries an internal asterisk.
- **Evidence:** Expert observation — "doesn't feel mandated for strategical concerns on a daily basis, then feels really uncomfortable when being asked."

**Pain #2: The Reverse-Engineering Trap** *(Process — HIGH)*
- **The pain:** Starts from product, works backward. Strategy becomes a mirror of what exists, not a lens on what should change.
- **Impact:** Blind spots on investor interests, macro-objectives, competitive dynamics, market evolution — anything the product doesn't already address.
- **Evidence:** Expert observation — "biased by the product itself, retro engineers strategy from the product" + "blind spots: investor's interests, macro-objectives."

**Pain #3: One Strategy Fits All** *(Process — HIGH)*
- **The pain:** Applies the same analysis regardless of situation type. No diagnostic layer.
- **Impact:** Sometimes accidentally fits, sometimes fundamentally misframes. Can't tell the difference.
- **Evidence:** Expert observation — "one strategy fits all."

**Pain #4: The Template Graveyard** *(Learning — MEDIUM)*
- **The pain:** Tried frameworks, got structure without interpretation guidance. "So what?" moment after filling in a BMC.
- **Impact:** Concludes strategy tools don't work. Burns the bridge to future attempts.
- **Evidence:** Inferred from lean persona pull-back forces + one-strategy-fits-all observation.

**Pain #5: The Loneliness of the Role** *(Emotional — MEDIUM)*
- **The pain:** Nobody thinks alongside her on strategy. The 5-minute gut-check call is the only strategic partnership.
- **Impact:** Echo-chamber analysis. Can't challenge own assumptions.
- **Evidence:** Inferred from Does/Feels — gut-check call as coping for isolation.

**Pain #6: Strategic/Tactical Confusion** *(Learning — MEDIUM)*
- **The pain:** Confuses strategic plan with tactical plan. Presents feature roadmap as strategy.
- **Impact:** Board or investors see through it. Credibility erodes.
- **Evidence:** Expert observation — "confusion between strategic plan and tactical plan."

**Summary:**
- **High Priority:** 3 (legitimacy void, reverse-engineering, one-fits-all)
- **Medium Priority:** 3 (template graveyard, loneliness, strategic/tactical confusion)
- **Low Priority:** 0

## Desired Gains

**Priority:** High = Must-have | Medium = Important | Low = Nice-to-have

**Gain #1: "I belong in this conversation"** *(Emotional — HIGH)*
- **Value:** Strategy stops feeling like someone else's job. Presents with ownership, not survival mode.
- **Success metric:** Language shifts from "I put together some thoughts on..." to "our strategy is..."

**Gain #2: "I know what I don't know"** *(Functional — HIGH)*
- **Value:** Coverage awareness. Can name which angles considered, which parked, and why.
- **Success metric:** When board asks a hard question, says "we considered that — here's why we parked it" instead of freezing.

**Gain #3: "This feels like thinking, not homework"** *(Emotional — HIGH)*
- **Value:** Curiosity flashes triggered reliably. Genuine exploration, not template-filling. Wants to come back.
- **Success metric:** Opens the Coach voluntarily for next decision — not because board demanded it.

**Gain #4: "I can do this in a week, not a quarter"** *(Functional — MEDIUM)*
- **Value:** Full assessment in 2-3 sessions under 12 hours. Fits between sprint reviews and customer calls.
- **Success metric:** Presentable output within 5 working days of part-time effort.

**Gain #5: "My team gets it too"** *(Social — MEDIUM)*
- **Value:** Strategic vocabulary and thinking transfer to the team. Strategy becomes shared, not solo.
- **Success metric:** A team member uses strategic language correctly in a meeting unprompted.

**Summary:**
- **High Priority:** 3 (belonging, coverage awareness, curiosity)
- **Medium Priority:** 2 (time efficiency, team transfer)
- **Low Priority:** 0

## Design Implications

### What This Means for the Strategy Coach Agent

**Based on this empathy map, we should:**

1. **Prioritize:** Legitimacy-conferring language and behavior. The Coach's first job is to make Nadia feel like she *should* be doing strategy — not to teach her frameworks. "You're the right person for this. Let me help you structure what you already know."

2. **Avoid:** Jargon-first interactions, academic tone, framework dumps, anything that triggers "this isn't for people like me." The impostor hum is always listening.

3. **Focus On:** Sustaining curiosity flashes. The entry point isn't "let's do strategy" — it's "let me show you something interesting about your situation." The Coach earns trust through insight, not instruction.

### Coach Agent Design Brief (from Pain → Gain map)

| Pain Point | Desired Gain | Coach Behavior |
|------------|--------------|----------------|
| Legitimacy void | "I belong in this conversation" | Confer legitimacy. "You're doing strategy — let me help you do it well." Never imply she should already know this. |
| Reverse-engineering trap | "I know what I don't know" | Surface what the product-backward view misses — *before* she discovers the blind spot in front of the board. |
| Template graveyard | "This feels like thinking, not homework" | Guide interpretation, not just structure. "Here's what your BMC is telling you" not "fill in this box." |
| One strategy fits all | Coverage + diagnostic | Ask "what kind of decision are you facing?" and route to the right approach. The diagnostic that Agent A provides. |
| Loneliness | Strategic sparring partner | Think alongside her, challenge gently, celebrate insights. The gut-check call, but sustained and structured. |
| Strategic/tactical confusion | Vocabulary + framing | Gently distinguish: "That's a strong tactical plan. Let me help you connect it to the strategic question your board is asking." |
