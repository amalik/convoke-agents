# Lean Persona: Strategic Practitioner

> **Remember:** This is a hypothesis until validated. Every insight below needs testing with real users.

## Executive Summary

**Job-to-be-Done:**
When I land on a consulting engagement or face a product inflection point, I need to rapidly assess the strategic situation and select the right analytical approach — so I produce a defensible recommendation in days, not weeks, without blind spots and with confidence I chose the right lens.

**Riskiest Assumptions:**
1. The integration layer (framework selection + synthesis) is the real gap, not framework execution
2. Structured diagnosis won't feel like overhead under time pressure (the 15-minute test)
3. A second practitioner can produce defensible output from the same workflows without mentoring

**Next Validation Steps:**
1. Test RA1 + RA2 during Vortex discovery (Convoke case + BP2 external case)
2. Test RA3 after discovery — peer runs workflows on a third case, expert reviews blind
3. All three must pass before Loom scaffolding

## Job-to-be-Done

### Primary Job
Rapidly assess a strategic situation, select the right analytical frameworks, and synthesize findings into a defensible recommendation — with confidence in both the diagnosis and the approach.

### Context
Mid-to-senior consultants (independent or firm-based) and product leaders facing strategic decisions. They have foundations in strategy frameworks but lack a structured process for selecting, combining, and synthesizing across frameworks. Their expertise is in application and judgment, not in any single framework.

### Frequency & Importance
- **Frequency:** Per engagement (2-6/year for consultants), at inflection points for product leaders. Unpredictable timing, high urgency when triggered.
- **Importance:** Mission-critical. A wrong diagnosis wastes the entire engagement. Choosing the wrong framework produces analysis that looks rigorous but answers the wrong question. This is the highest-leverage moment in any strategic engagement.

### Current Solution
Personal mental models + ad-hoc framework selection + consulting firm playbooks (if employed) + peer review when available. No structured integration layer exists.

---

## Current State Analysis

### How They Solve It Today
The practitioner relies on accumulated experience to select a framework (usually their favourite or most recent), runs the analysis, synthesizes in their head, and presents. Peer review — when it happens — serves as informal blind-spot detection. There is no systematic diagnostic step, no structured synthesis process, and no coverage verification.

### Pain Points with Current Solution
1. **Framework selection is gut-driven** — picks the lens by habit, not by diagnosis of situational fit
2. **Synthesis across frameworks is manual and unstructured** — convergence happens in the practitioner's head, quality depends entirely on individual skill
3. **Blind spot detection is absent** — no challenge mechanism for missing angles
4. **Knowledge stays trapped in the individual** — "it depends" is the only transfer mechanism for mentoring
5. **Time pressure forces shortcuts** — skip unfamiliar frameworks even when relevant
6. **Top-down without bottom-up** — strategic analysis built on assumptions about operational reality, never grounded in field evidence. The most dangerous anti-pattern because the output *looks* rigorous.

### Workarounds & Hacks
- Personal "pre-flight checklist" of angles to consider — informal, undocumented, varies per person
- Peer "challenge session" before presenting — manual blind-spot detection, depends on peer availability
- Early field visits or stakeholder interviews to ground top-down thinking — instinct, not process
- HYPOTHESIS: the bottom-up grounding workaround is what separates great strategists from good ones — and it's the least teachable part

### Cost of Current Solution
- **Time:** 3-5 days per strategic assessment (could be 1-2 with structured process)
- **Opportunity cost:** Recommendations that miss operational reality get challenged by implementers, requiring rework or losing credibility
- **Risk:** The top-down-only shortcut is invisible until it fails — the recommendation sounds right but doesn't survive contact with the field
- **Scaling cost:** Every new practitioner requires weeks of shadowing and mentoring to develop the integration judgment

## Problem Contexts

### When Does This Problem Occur?
- Week 1 of a new engagement — the window where strategic direction is set and hardest to change later
- At product inflection points — pivot decisions, market entry, repositioning (1-4 times/year)
- Pre-proposal or pre-pitch — demonstrating strategic thinking to win the engagement
- URGENCY SPIKES: when a client asks "what should we do?" and the practitioner hasn't yet formed a view

### Where Does This Problem Occur?
- **Physical:** Client site, home office, transit. Strategy thinking happens everywhere — formalized at a desk.
- **Digital:** Slide decks, whiteboard tools, personal notes, research documents. No single "strategy workspace."
- **State of mind:** High cognitive load. Absorbing a new domain, meeting new stakeholders, managing client expectations — all while trying to think clearly about strategic direction. The most mentally demanding phase of any engagement.

### Why Does This Problem Matter Now?
- Agentic AI makes the integration layer possible for the first time — can encode framework selection logic and synthesis reasoning, not just templates
- Consulting market commoditizing on frameworks — differentiation is now in integration and judgment
- Expert capacity is the bottleneck — can't mentor every practitioner personally
- HYPOTHESIS: market ready for "strategy-as-a-practice" tooling because building blocks (individual frameworks) are mature but the orchestration layer doesn't exist

### What Triggers the Need?
- New engagement signed — "I need a strategic assessment by Friday"
- Client escalation — "your recommendation doesn't match what we're seeing on the ground"
- Peer request — "can you review my analysis before I present?"
- Self-doubt moment — "am I looking at this from the right angle?"

## Forces & Anxieties

### Forces Pushing Toward Change
**What makes them want to switch?**
1. **The top-down trap** (HIGHEST) — recommendation built without bottom-up grounding gets challenged. Credibility damage is immediate.
2. **Blind spot anxiety** — "did I miss an angle?" haunts between analysis and presentation. No way to verify coverage.
3. **Mentoring bottleneck** — can't scale expertise through 1:1 mentoring. Every new consultant = weeks of shadowing.
4. **Time-to-recommendation pressure** — clients expect clarity in days. Ad-hoc process is slow and unrepeatable.
5. **Framework commoditization** — if every junior can run a SWOT, the differentiator is integration and judgment.

### Forces Pulling Back to Status Quo
**What makes them hesitate?**
1. **"My approach works"** — survivorship bias. Succeeded with personal method for years.
2. **Fear of rigidity** — a structured process might force a framework where intuition serves better.
3. **Time to adopt** — learning during active engagements feels risky. "I'll try it next time."
4. **Identity threat** — strategic judgment is professional identity. A tool that encodes it could feel like it diminishes expertise.

### Anxieties About Changing
- "What if the structured process produces generic output that doesn't match my client's nuance?"
- "What if I become dependent on the tool and lose my own judgment over time?"
- "What if my peers see me using an AI-assisted strategy tool and question my credibility?"
- "What if it works for textbook cases but breaks on messy, ambiguous situations where I earn my fee?"

### Habits That Need Breaking
- Starting with a favourite framework rather than diagnosing first
- Synthesizing in one's head rather than through structured convergence
- Skipping frameworks outside comfort zone
- Treating bottom-up validation as optional when time is short

## Success Criteria

### Desired Outcome
"I can produce a defensible strategic recommendation in 1-2 days instead of 3-5, with confidence that I've diagnosed the situation correctly, selected the right analytical lenses, and grounded my thinking in both top-down frameworks and bottom-up reality."

### Success Metrics
1. **Time-to-recommendation:** 1-2 days (vs. current 3-5 days)
2. **Framework coverage:** Every assessment explicitly considers diagnostic fit before selecting frameworks
3. **Blind spot reduction:** Practitioner can articulate which angles considered *and* which deliberately excluded with rationale
4. **Bottom-up grounding:** Every recommendation includes explicit `[KE-CONSUMABLE]` inputs — populated or flagged as assumptions
5. **Transferability:** Second practitioner follows same workflows and produces comparable quality without mentoring

### Time to Value
- **First use:** Must feel like it's accelerating thinking, not adding overhead. 15-minute test: if bureaucratic, adoption dies.
- **First engagement:** At least one insight the practitioner wouldn't have reached through habitual approach
- **Third engagement:** Practitioner's own judgment visibly sharper — the tool teaches, not just executes
- **Ongoing:** Other practitioners produce output the expert rates as "defensible"

### Acceptable Trade-offs
- 30-60 minutes of structured diagnosis to save days of rework
- Formalization of synthesis step (writing it down vs. head-only)
- Tool challenges initial framework choice — friction is the value
- Learning curve over 2-3 engagements before natural

### NOT Acceptable Trade-offs
- Adding overhead that doesn't improve recommendation quality
- Forcing rigid sequence when situation calls for a different path
- Generic, template-feeling output a client would see through
- Reducing practitioner autonomy — tool advises, practitioner decides
- Extensive setup or configuration before each engagement

## Assumptions to Validate

### Critical Assumptions (Test First)
1. **RA1: Integration gap is real** — practitioners struggle with framework selection and synthesis, not execution. If wrong → agents should be framework tutors, not integration orchestrators.
2. **RA2: Structured diagnosis passes the 15-minute test** — under real time pressure, practitioners find diagnostic step valuable, not overhead. If wrong → redesign for lighter touch.
3. **RA3: Transferability works** — workflows encode enough judgment for a second practitioner to produce defensible output without mentoring. If wrong → Coach agent scope must expand or encodability is questioned.

### Important Assumptions (Test Soon)
4. The top-down-only anti-pattern is the primary failure mode (not other strategic errors)
5. Framework selection logic can be encoded as context-sensitive orchestration
6. The 4 theoretical foundations (Porter, Wardley, BMC, Rumelt) are sufficient coverage for most engagements

### Nice-to-Validate (Test Later)
7. Third-engagement teaching effect is real (tool improves practitioner's judgment, not just output)
8. Peer credibility concern is overstated (practitioners won't hide tool use once they see quality)
9. Bottom-up grounding tags (`[KE-CONSUMABLE]`) create natural integration points when Forge ships

## Validation Plan

### Experiment 1: Integration vs. Execution Gap (RA1)
- **Hypothesis:** If the gap is integration, then the diagnostic step will surface at least 1 framework or angle the practitioner wouldn't have considered, and the practitioner will rate it as "valuable" not "redundant."
- **Method:** Self-discovery primary case (Convoke) + BP2 external case. Track whether diagnostic step adds unique value. Post-case debrief: "What did structured diagnosis add vs. your habitual approach?"
- **Success Criteria:** In both cases, at least 1 angle surfaced that practitioner wouldn't have reached. Rated as "valuable" not "redundant."
- **Timeline:** During Vortex discovery (Convoke case + BP2 external validation)
- **Decision:** Validated → integration orchestrator architecture confirmed. Invalidated → pivot toward framework execution support. Partial → hybrid.

### Experiment 2: The 15-Minute Test (RA2)
- **Hypothesis:** If structured diagnosis accelerates rather than burdens, then a practitioner under real time pressure will complete the diagnostic step in <30 minutes and rate it as "time well spent."
- **Method:** During Convoke case, time the diagnostic step. Post-completion rating: "saved time downstream / overhead / neutral." Repeat on BP2 case.
- **Success Criteria:** Completes in <30 minutes. Rated as "saved time" or "surfaced something I'd have missed." Rated "overhead" → fail.
- **Timeline:** During Vortex discovery
- **Decision:** Validated → workflow design confirmed. Invalidated → redesign diagnostic step lighter and faster. Both cases fail → fundamental rethink of mandatory A step.

### Experiment 3: Transferability (RA3)
- **Hypothesis:** If workflows encode enough judgment, then a practitioner without the expert's synthesis experience can produce output the expert rates as "defensible."
- **Method:** After Convoke + BP2 cases complete, have a willing peer or junior consultant run Strategy workflows on a third case. Expert reviews output blind.
- **Success Criteria:** Expert rates output as "defensible — I'd present with minor adjustments" or better. "Needs fundamental rework" → fail.
- **Timeline:** After Strategy perimeter discovery complete, before Loom scaffolding
- **Decision:** Validated → scaffold via Loom, Coach design confirmed. Invalidated → expand Coach scope or question encodability (pivot trigger).
