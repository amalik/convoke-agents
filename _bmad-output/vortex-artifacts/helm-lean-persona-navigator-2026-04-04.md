# Lean Persona: Strategic Navigator

> **Remember:** This is a hypothesis until validated. This is our **lowest-confidence persona** — built from the expert's observation of product teams, not from direct research with novice teams. Isla's empathy validation is a prerequisite before designing the Coach agent.

## Executive Summary

**Job-to-be-Done:**
When my product faces a critical decision — launch, pivot, competitive threat, or growth stall — I need to think through it strategically rather than reactively, so I make a decision I can defend to my team and stakeholders, even though I have scattered strategy knowledge but no integration framework and no confidence to act on it.

**Riskiest Assumptions:**
1. Scattered knowledge is the norm, not zero knowledge (NRA1)
2. A Coach agent can bridge the confidence gap — it's about guidance quality, not human trust (NRA2)
3. Strategy squeezed into margins still produces defensible output (NRA3)

**Next Validation Steps:**
1. Isla empathy validation — interview or observe novice product teams to confirm persona accuracy
2. Test NRA2 during BP2 external perspective check — use a novice-appropriate scenario
3. Test NRA3 by designing workflows that produce value in 2-3 hour sessions, not multi-day blocks

---

## Job-to-be-Done

### Primary Job
Think through critical product decisions strategically rather than reactively — using whatever strategic knowledge the team has, filling gaps they don't realize exist, and building enough confidence to defend the analysis to stakeholders.

### Context
Product teams at inflection points who have fragments of strategic knowledge (a business school module on Porter, a blog post on Wardley, a SWOT they ran once) but no integration framework and no confidence their analysis is rigorous enough. The problem isn't zero knowledge — it's scattered knowledge with no way to connect it or verify coverage.

### Frequency & Importance
- **Frequency:** At product inflection points (2-4/year). Triggered ~50% by external pressure (board, investors, stakeholders) and ~50% by internal recognition (pattern of reactive decisions).
- **Importance:** Very important to mission-critical. Decisions get made regardless — by gut, loudest voice, or default. The cost of bad strategic decisions compounds silently.

### Current Solution
Google searches for templates, partially filled canvases, informal colleague opinions, skipping strategy entirely, or occasionally hiring a consultant. No structured process.

---

## Current State Analysis

### How They Solve It Today
Download a BMC or SWOT template, fill it in partially, get stuck on interpretation, move on. Ask a smart colleague. Skip strategy and frame decisions as "data-driven" to avoid the conversation. Hire a consultant for big decisions (expensive, knowledge leaves with them).

### Pain Points with Current Solution
1. **Don't know what they don't know** — can't assess whether analysis is missing critical angles
2. **Template trap** — templates give structure but no guidance on interpretation or what to do with output
3. **Confidence gap** — not sure analysis is rigorous enough to present to board or investors
4. **No synthesis path** — might run two analyses but can't connect them into a coherent view
5. **Vocabulary barrier** — stakeholders use strategy language ("moat," "positioning," "value chain") and the team can't engage at that level

### Workarounds & Hacks
- Outsource to consultant for big decisions (expensive, creates dependency)
- Copy competitors' strategies without understanding the reasoning
- Frame decisions as "data-driven" to avoid the strategy conversation
- One team member becomes unofficial "strategy person" through self-study — fragile single point of failure

### Cost of Current Solution
- **Money:** Consulting fees ($5-50k per engagement) for intermittent strategic input
- **Time:** Hours spent on template exercises that produce obvious insights
- **Opportunity cost:** Strategic decisions made reactively compound into misaligned products and wasted build effort
- **Credibility:** Team can't engage in strategic conversations with board/investors at the expected level

## Problem Contexts

### When Does This Problem Occur?
- Board meetings approaching, funding rounds, annual planning
- Competitive threat lands, growth stalls
- Almost always *reactive* — the need is urgent before they recognize it

### Where Does This Problem Occur?
- Meeting rooms, shared docs, slide decks being built the night before a presentation
- High stress, low time, multiple stakeholders with opinions
- Strategy gets squeezed into margins between sprint planning, feature work, customer calls

### Why Does This Problem Matter Now?
- Agentic AI can provide the guided process a consultant would, at zero marginal cost
- Product teams are expected to "think strategically" but not trained to
- Investor and stakeholder expectations for strategic clarity are rising
- The gap between "we should have a strategy" and "here's how to build one" feels vast and intimidating

### What Triggers the Need?
- Board asks "what's your strategy?" and the honest answer is "we're figuring it out"
- Competitor does something unexpected — no framework to assess or respond
- Growth stalls and the team realizes they've been building without strategic direction
- New hire (often senior) asks "where's the strategy doc?" and there isn't one

---

## Forces & Anxieties

### Forces Pushing Toward Change
**What makes them want to switch?**
1. **External accountability** (HIGHEST) — board, investors, or stakeholders demand strategic clarity
2. **Competitor shock** — unexpected competitive move with no framework to respond
3. **Repeated reactive decisions** — team recognizes the firefighting pattern
4. **Hired consultant insight** — saw what structured thinking looks like, want to replicate without the cost

### Forces Pulling Back to Status Quo
**What makes them hesitate?**
1. **"Strategy is for big companies"** — belief their stage doesn't warrant formal strategy
2. **Time scarcity** — "we barely have time to build, let alone strategize"
3. **Intimidation** — strategy frameworks feel academic and inaccessible
4. **Past attempts failed** — tried SWOT once, produced obvious insights, concluded strategy tools don't add value

### Anxieties About Changing
- "What if the analysis tells us something we don't want to hear?"
- "What if we spend time on strategy and the board says it's not rigorous enough anyway?"
- "What if this is just another template exercise producing nothing actionable?"
- "What if our team argues more when we try to be structured instead of just deciding?"

### Habits That Need Breaking
- Making strategic decisions in the last 30 minutes of a meeting about something else
- Treating strategy as a document to produce, not a thinking process to follow
- Deferring strategic thinking to "when we have time" (which never comes)
- Equating data analysis with strategic analysis

## Success Criteria

### Desired Outcome
"We can walk into a board meeting or strategy discussion and present a structured analysis that we understand and can defend — not something a consultant wrote for us."

### Success Metrics
1. **Confidence to present:** Team rates own output as "I'd present this" (vs. "I hope nobody asks hard questions")
2. **Coverage awareness:** Team can name which angles they've considered and which they've parked
3. **Actionability:** Analysis produces at least 2 concrete decisions or direction changes
4. **Time investment:** Full strategic assessment in <1 week of part-time work (2-3 hour sessions, not multi-day blocks)
5. **Independence:** By the third use, team runs the process without needing the Coach for every step

### Time to Value
- **First 15 minutes:** "Oh, *this* is what strategy looks like" — aha moment, not lecture
- **First session:** At least one insight the team hadn't articulated
- **First full run:** Output presentable to stakeholders without embarrassment
- **Third use:** Team uses strategic vocabulary naturally and asks better questions

### Acceptable Trade-offs
- Spend 2-3 hours per session over a week to get a defensible strategic view
- Accept that the process will surface uncomfortable truths about product direction
- Accept that the Coach guides rather than decides — the team owns the output
- Accept starting with a simpler framework when the full toolkit would be overwhelming

### NOT Acceptable Trade-offs
- Academic tone or jargon that makes the team feel dumber
- Output only a strategy expert can interpret
- Process requiring a facilitator to run
- Analysis that produces "interesting" but not actionable insights
- Adding to the team's workload without visible decision-making value

## Assumptions to Validate

### Critical Assumptions (Test First)
1. **NRA1: Scattered knowledge is the norm** — most novice teams have fragments (Porter module, SWOT attempt) not zero knowledge. If wrong → Coach needs to teach fundamentals, not just integrate fragments.
2. **NRA2: Coach agent bridges the confidence gap** — guidance quality matters more than human source. Confidence gap occurs in both AI-assisted and human contexts (initial evidence from expert observation). If wrong → Coach pattern has fundamental limits.
3. **NRA3: Strategy in margins works** — workflows must produce defensible output in 2-3 hour sessions squeezed between other work. If wrong → workflows need redesign for the novice's time constraints.

### Important Assumptions (Test Soon)
4. The vocabulary barrier is a symptom, not the root cause — teaching terms without teaching thinking is useless
5. Past SWOT failures were caused by lack of interpretation guidance, not by the framework itself
6. The "strategy is for big companies" belief dissolves when the team sees immediate applicability

### Nice-to-Validate (Test Later)
7. Independence by third use is achievable (not just hoped for)
8. Teams that learn strategy through the Coach become better at recognizing when they need the full practitioner-level agents
9. The Coach pattern transfers to other domains (Discovery Coach, Readiness Coach)

## Validation Plan

### Experiment 1: Knowledge Baseline (NRA1)
- **Hypothesis:** If scattered knowledge is the norm, then when novice product teams are asked "what strategy frameworks have you used?", at least 60% will name 1-2 frameworks but describe incomplete or failed application.
- **Method:** Isla empathy interviews with 3-5 product teams. Structured question: "Walk me through the last time you tried to think strategically about your product."
- **Success Criteria:** Majority have fragments (some framework exposure) not zero knowledge. If majority have truly zero → Coach scope expands to include fundamentals.
- **Timeline:** Isla empathy validation phase
- **Decision:** Validated → Coach integrates fragments. Invalidated → Coach adds foundational teaching layer.

### Experiment 2: Coach Confidence Effect (NRA2)
- **Hypothesis:** If the confidence gap is about guidance quality, then a novice team using the Coach agent will rate their confidence in presenting strategic output ≥6/10 (vs. estimated ≤3/10 baseline without guidance).
- **Method:** During BP2 external check, use a novice-appropriate scenario. Pre/post confidence rating.
- **Success Criteria:** Confidence rating ≥6/10 post-Coach. If <4/10 → Coach doesn't bridge the gap.
- **Timeline:** BP2 external perspective check
- **Decision:** Validated → Coach design confirmed. Invalidated → investigate what's missing (facilitation? validation? human reassurance?).

### Experiment 3: Margins Viability (NRA3)
- **Hypothesis:** If strategy in margins works, then a novice team can complete a full strategic assessment across 3-4 sessions of 2-3 hours each (within one week) and produce output rated "presentable" by the expert.
- **Method:** After Coach workflows designed, pilot with a willing product team on a real decision. Track session count, total hours, and output quality.
- **Success Criteria:** Completed in ≤4 sessions, ≤12 total hours. Expert rates output as "presentable with minor coaching." If requires >20 hours or expert rates "not presentable" → fail.
- **Timeline:** After Strategy perimeter scaffolded, before general release
- **Decision:** Validated → workflow timing confirmed. Invalidated → redesign for shorter, more focused sessions or accept that novice path requires more time investment.

## Relationship to Companion Persona

| Dimension | Strategic Practitioner | Strategic Navigator (this persona) |
|-----------|----------------------|-----------------------------------|
| **Knowledge** | Deep integration across frameworks | Scattered fragments, no integration |
| **Confidence** | High (may have blind spots) | Low (may have more knowledge than they think) |
| **Time** | 3-5 days focused strategic work | 2-3 hour sessions squeezed into margins |
| **Entry point** | Direct to core agents (A, D, E) | Coach mediates, explains, builds literacy |
| **Success metric** | Faster, more defensible recommendations | Able to do strategy at all — and defend it |
| **Teaching effect** | Sharpens existing judgment | Builds new capability |

The Coach agent bridges these two personas. Over time, a Navigator who uses the Coach repeatedly should develop enough literacy to interact directly with core agents — graduating from novice to practitioner-level engagement.
